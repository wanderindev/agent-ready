## 1. Purpose and relationship to the Phase 1 synthesis

This document is a companion to the Phase 1 synthesis, not a revision of it. The synthesis stays as written — v1, dated, calibrated to the audit phase. The addendum captures the fixing-phase methodology Phase 2 surfaced, distilled from twenty-one Phase 2 sessions and twenty-four cross-session register entries over four weeks of fix-execution.

The synthesis's voice is *"here is how to run an audit on a codebase you're about to hand to autonomous agents."* The addendum's voice is *"here is how to execute the resulting backlog, with autonomous agents on the tractable subset and human pairing on the rest."* The two documents describe two halves of one pipeline: audit-to-autonomy. The synthesis articulates the first half; the addendum articulates the second.

Phase 2 produced more than confirmation of Phase 1's hypotheses. The synthesis's §10 anticipated five or six falsifiability questions; Phase 2 answered some of them and surfaced methodology of its own that the synthesis didn't and couldn't anticipate. The register entries are the primary evidence — twenty-four entries across four weeks — and the agent-friendly outcomes log is the §10 data: sixteen autonomous-mode runs, sixteen merged PRs, zero reviewer interventions on code substance.

### Explicit non-goals

1. **Not a v2 of the synthesis.** The synthesis remains as written. Future revisions to either document should stay version-distinct rather than merged into one document that loses compositional integrity.
2. **Not the public thesis articulation.** That deliverable follows this one.
3. **Not the artifact updates themselves.** §4 specifies what needs updating in `docs/methodology/`; the next session executes them, mirror of the artifact sessions that followed the Phase 1 synthesis.
4. **Not a re-synthesis with Phase 2 added.** The reader has read the synthesis; the addendum references rather than restates.
5. **Not a finished methodology.** Same v1 status. Repo 2 is the test plan, not the confirmation.

## 2. The §10 hypotheses, tested

The synthesis's §10 named several falsifiability questions. Phase 2 touched on each; some are answered within PIC's scope, some are touched but inconclusive, and some are by definition cross-codebase and remain open.

**The override-rate / autonomy-execution question** is the most direct test Phase 2 ran. The synthesis described PIC's Phase 1 override rate as *"on the order of twice across roughly 110 issues"* and flagged it as recollection rather than measurement. Phase 2 measured deliberately. Sixteen autonomous-mode runs, sixteen merged PRs, zero reviewer interventions on code substance, four agent-vs-brief events (three of them under the pre-tightening brief in F-1, one as F-3.C's flag-and-follow). Within PIC, the autonomy-execution claim is confirmed at the strongest possible evidentiary bar. The caveats from the F-4 cumulative register entry stay attached: one orchestrator, one codebase, one model family (Opus 4.7), sixteen issues from one audit's `agent-friendly:yes` pool, no `borderline` or `no` labels tested under autonomous mode. The within-PIC autonomy claim is confirmed; the cross-codebase claim is still open.

**The agent-friendly-gradation question** asked whether the flat `agent-friendly:yes` label should be split into `agent-friendly:clear` and `agent-friendly:supervised`, or replaced with a richer description schema. Phase 2 produces the decisive evidence: the flat label is sufficient *when the brief carries the session-06 disciplines*. The F-2.2 vs F-1 comparison isolated brief-tightness from scope — same multi-file sweep shape, different brief tightness, three ambiguities under the pre-tightening brief versus zero under the tightened brief. Brief-tightness, not label gradation, is the load-bearing variable. A graded label would have offered no additional signal; the prompt-construction discipline was doing all the work. This is the strongest single concrete finding the addendum carries.

**The cross-codebase generalization questions** — whether the prompt-construction logic generalizes, whether partial-correction debt is PIC-specific, whether the auto-approve fence holds on different production topology — are by definition not answerable from one codebase. Phase 2 operationalized the fence concretely (see §3 pattern 7); the operationalization is portable, but its sufficiency on different topology is repo-2 work. Phase 2 did not directly test partial-correction debt prevalence; if anything, the fact that several of the Phase 2 fix PRs (sessions 02, 04, F-2.3, F-4.G) were themselves partial-correction sweeps suggests the pattern is at least as visible in the fixing phase as in the auditing phase.

**The prompt-13-style ground-truth question** — whether the snapshot-as-prompt mechanism generalizes — Phase 2 didn't directly test. The Phase 2 kickoff session re-confirmed issue state at session start, but informally rather than as a structured snapshot session. Whether the synthesis-time snapshot ritual is needed at the fixing-phase boundary is a question repo 2 will answer.

**The cross-session-transcript artifact question** — whether the live cross-session conversation is worth preserving deliberately — Phase 2 answered with a "yes, formalized as a register." See §5 for the register's evaluation as an instrument. The Phase 2 register did the job the synthesis's open question wanted answered: it captured patterns that would otherwise have lived only in recollection, with discipline-and-slip honesty.

## 3. Fixing-phase methodology

Phase 2 surfaced seven patterns the synthesis didn't and couldn't anticipate. Each is stated below with its register evidence and a falsifiable claim — what would invalidate it as a general pattern when tested on repo 2.

### 3.1 The cleanup-then-refactor pattern, with its bundle-vs-split diagnostic

When a backlog issue has both a *manifested-corruption* half (live production damage) and a *structural-code-fix* half (the bug that caused the corruption), the orchestrator faces a sequencing decision: ship them as one PR, or split. PIC produced both shapes. Sessions 02-03 split #99 — session 02 cleaned articles 39/40 in production and added a safety-net refusal; session 03 then did the raw-SQL → ORM refactor as a clean follow-up. Session 04 bundled cluster B — the email contract flip *was* the broken-in-prod fix; splitting would have meant deploying a wrapper twice. The diagnostic question the register surfaced after session 04: *"if you shipped only the manifested fix, would the structural fix still be a follow-up worth doing?"* For #99: yes (the raw-SQL → ORM refactor was meaningful even after the data was cleaned). For cluster B: no (the wrapper would already be the new wrapper). Register evidence: the cleanup-then-refactor pattern entry (2026-05-25, backfilled) and its bound entry (2026-05-25, session 04). **Falsifiable as general** if a repo-2 manifested-corruption issue has a structural half that doesn't separate cleanly under the diagnostic — for instance, if the manifested fix forces a partial structural change that's neither shippable-alone nor cleanly-bundleable. That would not invalidate the pattern but would refine the diagnostic.

### 3.2 The F-1 through F-4 ramp-up structure as an autonomous-agent validation framework

The synthesis predicted that frontend would be the autonomous-agent track and named #117 as the cleanest entry. Phase 2 turned that prediction into a four-step ramp-up: F-1 single-shot on the named entry (#117 alone, isolated worktree), F-2 serial (three more sequentially), F-3 small-batch parallelism (three concurrent on independent files), F-4 full track (the remaining nine issues across two waves of five and four). The structure's contribution is not just operational — it's methodological. Each step's evidence is interpretable in isolation: F-1's single data point established whether autonomous-mode worked at all; F-2's three sequential runs tested whether the brief-template held under repetition; F-3's three concurrent runs tested whether parallelism degraded per-agent quality; F-4's two waves tested whether the parallelism scales and whether the brief-template generalizes across shape (a JSX restructure, an infrastructure-only ship, a dead-code removal). Parallelism escalates only when prior data justifies the next step. Register evidence: the original F-1 → F-4 design entry (2026-05-24), each phase's cumulative entry (F-1 outcome 2026-05-26, F-2 cumulative 2026-05-26, F-3 cumulative 2026-05-27, F-4 cumulative 2026-05-27). **Falsifiable as general** if a repo 2 produces a clean F-1 but a degraded F-3 — for instance, if ambiguity events rise sharply when concurrency exceeds two. That would argue for the ramp-up framework with a capped ceiling rather than the open-ended ramp PIC's data implies.

### 3.3 The brief-tightness disciplines

Phase 2's single most consequential finding: per-agent quality under autonomous execution depends on the brief, not on the issue. Six concrete techniques emerged from F-1's three ambiguity events and the F-2 through F-4 runs that operated without them. **(1) Codebase-fact verification against source, not memory** — F-1's brief asserted that `AdminLayout` provided a `ToastProvider`; the source had no such provider. Every codebase-fact claim in subsequent briefs was traceable to a specific `file:line` the orchestrator read at brief-writing time. **(2) Count pre-resolution** — F-1's "22 catch blocks" included one canonical-pattern block that was already conformant; the fix-count was 21. Every subsequent sweep brief pre-resolved the count interpretation. **(3) Worktree-setup pre-resolution** — F-1 discovered that fresh worktrees have no `node_modules` and "no `npm install`" is unsatisfiable; the symlink-from-main-checkout resolution was documented in every subsequent brief. **(4) Line-region ownership declaration** for same-file parallel runs — F-3 onward (most explicitly F-4.G + F-4.I on `EditDrawer.jsx`). **(5) Exhaustive grep enumeration for dead-code removal** — F-4.F caught an `AppShell.jsx:23` reference the brief omitted but the issue body had listed. **(6) Infrastructure-only-shipping pre-resolution** — F-4.H landed a hook with no consumers; the brief had to pre-resolve "why ship without consumers?" or the agent might over-engineer toward immediate wiring. The brief is also the *reconciliation* between issue body and source: when source contradicts the issue body (F-3.C's 12-vs-8 hero count), the brief explicitly states the correction rather than silently overriding. Register evidence: the brief-was-wrong-about-codebase-fact entry (2026-05-26), worktree node_modules entry (2026-05-26), brief-correcting-issue-body entry (2026-05-27), brief-writing refinements at N=16 entry (2026-05-27). The decisive comparison is F-1 (pre-tightening brief, three ambiguities) vs F-2.2 (post-tightening brief, same sweep shape, zero ambiguities). **Falsifiable as general** if a repo with weaker pattern-density — no canonical patterns living in the same codebase — produces higher ambiguity rates even with tight briefs. That would argue brief-tightness is necessary but not sufficient; the codebase's own self-similarity contributes too.

### 3.4 The agent-vs-brief disagreement taxonomy

Across sixteen autonomous runs, four distinct shapes of brief-vs-source disagreement emerged. Each shape requires different agent behavior; calling them all "override events" would have hidden the actually-informative signal. **Shape 1 — brief said exclude, source implied include:** the agent overrides and includes (F-1's AdminCalendar `.catch(console.error)` shorthand, where the brief excluded shorthand-form catches but the issue body's count implicitly included them). **Shape 2 — brief was factually wrong about the codebase:** the agent follows source over brief (F-1's `ToastProvider`-that-isn't). **Shape 3 — brief correct for the primary case but under-specified for a sub-case:** the agent follows the brief AND flags the tension in the PR description (F-3.C's `ExcursionsHero`/`ToursHero` lazy-loading — above-the-fold on their own routes, below-the-fold from the homepage perspective the brief implicitly assumed). **Shape 4 — positive judgment within scope:** the agent extends within the issue's intent and flags transparently (F-2.1's `EMPTY_CART` constant extraction; F-4.B's typo fix and misleading-line drop during the i18n sweep; F-4.F's `AppShell.jsx:23` addition; F-4.I's read-only fallback). Each shape is correct in its context: shape 1 requires expanded strict-reading; shape 2 requires override-and-flag; shape 3 requires follow-and-flag; shape 4 requires extend-within-intent-and-flag. The §10 measurement should track these as four distinct event types rather than collapsing them into a single "override rate." For PIC's data: 4/16 = 25% rate of shape-4 events, all operator-ratified; shapes 1, 2, 3 occurred once each, all in or under-tightening briefs. Register evidence: the three-shape taxonomy entry (2026-05-27) and the fourth-shape agent-judgment-above-minimum entry (2026-05-27). **Falsifiable as general** if repo 2 surfaces shapes the taxonomy doesn't cover — for instance, agent disagreeing with the issue's premise rather than the brief's wording. That would argue the taxonomy is incomplete, not wrong. The taxonomy's framing should remain provisional until repo 2's data is in.

### 3.5 The failure-mode escape hatch pattern

The synthesis named the auto-approve fence as the highest-value single decision in the methodology. Phase 2 surfaced a complementary discipline: the *failure-mode escape hatch* in the brief itself. When the brief's primary path is blocked — for instance, the operation the brief asks for is structurally impossible — a pre-specified "stop and surface as draft, with the alternative if the issue body anticipates one" instruction produces sophisticated, value-positive artifacts at the stop point rather than stalls or scope-expansions. F-4.I (PR #175) is the canonical evidence. The brief asked for a working `handleSave` on `OutlineEditor`; the agent's source verification revealed no backend endpoint exists for editable outlines; the brief explicitly anticipated this case (*"STOP and surface in draft PR"*). The agent followed it, found the read-only alternative the issue body itself anticipated, removed the dead `handleSave` that would have blanked article content if ever wired, opened a draft PR. The operator ratified the alternative and merged. One draft in sixteen runs (6.25% rate); zero stalls; zero scope-expansions. Briefs without escape hatches risk one of two failure modes — agent stalls with no output, or agent inappropriately expands scope to unblock — both more costly than a clean draft. Register evidence: the failure-mode escape hatch validated entry (2026-05-27). **Falsifiable as general** if a repo-2 escape-hatch-equipped brief produces a stall or an inappropriate scope expansion rather than a clean draft. That would argue the escape hatch needs additional structure — for instance, requiring the brief to state the alternative path explicitly rather than relying on the issue body to contain one.

### 3.6 Same-file non-adjacent parallelism

The synthesis's parallelization-by-shape framing (§6) assumed file independence as the structural rule for concurrent autonomous runs. Phase 2 demonstrated that file independence is *sufficient but not necessary*. F-4.G (issue #118, ResearchEditor at lines 40-117 of `EditDrawer.jsx`) and F-4.I (#120, OutlineEditor at line 654+) ran concurrently on the same file. The two regions were 500+ lines apart; each brief declared its line-region ownership explicitly and designated the other region as read-only-for-reference. Git's three-way merge auto-resolved the non-adjacent edits cleanly; no code-level operator intervention was required. The structural rule, refined: *no two concurrent agents edit the same lines or adjacent lines*. The brief-template addition: explicit line-region ownership declaration in the form of "you own lines X-Y; do not modify lines Z+; if your changes require touching lines outside X-Y, flag and stop." This expands the parallelism pool. Issues that touch shared infrastructure files become candidate-parallel as long as the work decomposes into non-adjacent regions. Register evidence: same-file non-adjacent parallelism works entry (2026-05-27). **Falsifiable as general** if a more tightly-coupled codebase — heavy shared imports, deeply-nested closures sharing state across nominally-separate sections, frequent same-line edits at imports or exports — produces conflicts that git can't auto-merge even with non-adjacent target regions. That would argue line-region independence is necessary but the line-region size threshold is codebase-specific, and the brief should specify a generous buffer.

### 3.7 The three-layer auto-approve fence operationalization

The synthesis specified the auto-approve fence at the principle level: reversible-vs-irreversible × production-vs-non-production. Phase 2 specified the enforcement mechanism, at three layers. **Layer 1 — `.claude/settings.json` `permissions.deny` rules:** the structural gate that fires before the operator even sees the prompt. PIC's L1 covers prod DB hostnames, the rotated AVNS_ credential prefix, force-push variants, push-to-main, `gh pr merge*`, prod registry push, bare alembic, mass deletion, `.env*` writes. **Layer 2 — in-the-loop skills (`database-ops`, `deploy`) as gates for prod access:** every prod-touching operation routes through a named skill rather than a bare command. The skill itself surfaces for approval; the named pattern is what the operator approves, not the underlying invocation. **Layer 3 — "Production touch: yes / no — gated by:" line in every PR description:** the audit-trail record that survives even if L1 and L2 are bypassed by accident. The three layers compensate for each other's failure modes — L1 catches commands the operator doesn't see; L2 names the operations the operator approves once-per-skill rather than once-per-command; L3 records the choice for any future reader. A consequence the register surfaces: L1's push-to-main deny rule interacts with the parallel-docs-editing pattern to produce conflicts on the outcomes log; the cost is small (the merge-main-into-branch resolution template is the operator's routine), and option (b) — accept the conflicts — was the right framing at F-3 design time. Register evidence: the fence operationalization entry (2026-05-24), sequential-flip discipline entry (2026-05-26). **Falsifiable as general** if a repo-2 production topology produces a class of risk none of the three layers catches — for instance, message-queue writes, workflow-engine state mutations, or eventually-consistent storage commits that the deny rules don't pattern-match. That would argue for an L4 (a vendor-specific deny block) or for refining L2's skill-mediation to cover more operation classes.

## 4. Implications for the methodology artifacts

The next session executes the artifact updates. This section specifies them concretely, with each item arguing for inclusion and the rough shape of the change.

### 4.1 Prompt-template

`docs/methodology/prompt-template.md` is the most directly affected artifact. The brief-tightness disciplines from §3.3 belong as either snippet additions or required-fill slots: codebase-fact verification, count pre-resolution, worktree-setup pre-resolution, line-region ownership declaration (for same-file parallel runs), exhaustive grep enumeration (for dead-code removal), infrastructure-only-shipping pre-resolution. The failure-mode escape hatch from §3.5 belongs as a snippet that any brief-writer can drop in when the primary path has a known-blocked alternative; the template should make it easy to specify both the stop trigger and the alternative if the issue body anticipates one. The three-shape and four-shape agent-vs-brief taxonomy from §3.4 belongs as a paragraph that every autonomous-agent brief includes — agents need to recognize which shape they're in before responding. Sequence: the artifact-update session writes these as template additions, with PIC Phase 2 prompts (sessions 06-21) as the working examples.

### 4.2 Cross-cutting checklist

`docs/methodology/cross-cutting-checklist.md` is the §8 disposition checklist. Phase 2 didn't surface new patterns to add to the existing six; the brief-tightness disciplines and the agent-vs-brief taxonomy are about prompt-construction, not about findings-to-look-for. The recommendation is to NOT extend the checklist with Phase 2's findings. The taxonomy belongs in a separate reference document (§4.5 below), not as a checklist item. The checklist's scope — "things to actively look for during area audits" — is auditing-phase work; Phase 2's findings are fixing-phase methodology. Conflating them would dilute the checklist's purpose.

### 4.3 Conventions doc

`docs/methodology/conventions.md` names two load-bearing practices: prompt preservation and cross-session register maintenance. Phase 2's cleanup-then-refactor pattern (§3.1) is a candidate for a third convention — *"when an issue has both a manifested half and a structural half, apply the bundle-vs-split diagnostic before deciding the PR shape."* The argument for inclusion: it's a cross-session decision pattern that lives outside any single prompt and shapes how subsequent sessions are run. The argument against: it's a specific instance of a more general "decide PR scope before drafting the brief" practice that may not need its own convention entry. The recommendation is to include it, with explicit framing: the diagnostic is the rule; bundling vs splitting is the outcome of the rule, not a separate principle. This belongs in conventions because it's discipline-not-gateable — no skill is in the loop when a brief-writer is deciding PR shape. The same-file non-adjacent parallelism rule (§3.6) is also gate-not-buildable in any obvious way; consider adding it as a third convention or folding into the prompt-template's line-region ownership snippet.

### 4.4 Reference-document-types

The `docs/methodology/reference-document-types/` directory specs the document shapes that audit areas produce. Phase 2 adds two reusable shapes that future fixing phases will produce: the **agent-friendly-outcomes log** and the **cross-session register itself**. The outcomes log is structurally a table with nine columns and an append-on-PR-open convention; its falsifiability hook is the synthesis §10 question. The register is structurally a table with six columns plus the slip-and-backfill discipline. Both are already in use; the recommendation is to add their format specs to `reference-document-types/` so repo 2's operator can adopt them on day one rather than evolve them.

### 4.5 New artifact: the agent-vs-brief disagreement taxonomy

The four-shape taxonomy from §3.4 is the strongest candidate for a net-new methodology artifact. It's distinct enough that folding it into the prompt-template would bury it; it's general enough that every autonomous-agent brief should reference it; it's likely to evolve as repo 2 produces new shapes. Recommendation: create `docs/methodology/agent-vs-brief-taxonomy.md` as a standalone reference document. The prompt-template's snippet for autonomous-agent briefs then links to it rather than restating it. The taxonomy doc captures the four shapes, the agent behavior each requires, the PIC-Phase-2 evidence per shape, and the falsifiability hook (what would invalidate the taxonomy as exhaustive).

### 4.6 Possible new artifact: the F-1 through F-4 ramp-up framework

The four-step ramp-up structure (§3.2) is a methodology contribution distinct from the brief-template — it specifies how to *sequence* autonomous-agent execution against a backlog, not how to write any single brief. Two arguments for a standalone artifact: (a) it's specific to fixing-phase work and the prompt-template is shape-agnostic; (b) repo 2 will want a recipe to follow rather than reconstructing the structure from this addendum. Argument against: a standalone artifact might over-prescribe a structure that's only validated on one codebase. Recommendation: defer the standalone artifact until repo 2 confirms the structure transfers. For repo 2, this addendum's §3.2 serves as the recipe; if it works, the next-pilot synthesis builds the artifact.

## 5. The cross-session register, examined

The register was the primary instrument for capturing Phase 2's fixing-phase methodology. The synthesis's §10 named the cross-session conversation transcript as an open-question artifact; Phase 2 was its first real test.

**What worked.** Twenty-four entries across four weeks; patterns surfaced that would otherwise have lived only in recollection. The brief-tightness disciplines (§3.3) crystallized only because the register captured the F-1 ambiguity events as distinct entries, which made the F-2.2 vs F-1 comparison possible. The four-shape agent-vs-brief taxonomy (§3.4) emerged only because three earlier shapes were already on record when F-3.C produced the fourth. The sequential-flip discipline (§3.7) is visible only because the register captured the #152/#153 docs-overlap incident as a methodology event rather than treating it as a one-off ops friction. The discipline that the register-writer's job is to convert friction into evidence — rather than route around it — held cleanly from session 04 onward.

**What didn't.** The four-row backfill between sessions 02 and 03 was a methodology slip, acknowledged in the register itself as its own honest entry. The discipline of real-time entry was harder to maintain in early Phase 2 sessions than later ones — partly because the early sessions were still operating under the prior pair-mode patterns and the register's role wasn't yet load-bearing, partly because the operator and the orchestrator were both learning what a register entry should contain. By session 06 onward the discipline held; the slip-and-backfill row is itself the methodology's record that it slipped and recovered.

**What this implies for repo 2.** The register's format is sound — six columns, chronological append, with the slip-and-backfill convention as the honesty mechanism. The discipline of real-time entry should be part of every fixing-session's closing ritual rather than an after-the-fact append. The recommendation is to consider whether the Phase 2 brief-template should require an inline "register update appended? Y/N" checkbox before session closure — a structural gate rather than a stated discipline. The synthesis's gate-not-guideline meta-principle applies: the convention slipped because no skill was in the loop. If a skill can be in the loop at session closure, the slip won't recur.

## 6. Open questions for repo 2

Same role as the synthesis's §10, but for fixing-phase methodology. What Phase 2 can't answer that repo 2 will. Each item framed falsifiably.

**Does the F-1 through F-4 ramp-up structure generalize to a codebase the same operator didn't audit?** PIC's audit and PIC's fix execution shared an orchestrator and a memory of the audit. Repo 2 either has a different orchestrator or a fresher operator at the fixing-phase boundary. If F-1 produces a clean run but F-2 onward degrades — for instance, more ambiguity events because the operator's brief-tightness depends on remembering audit decisions — that would argue the ramp-up structure needs an explicit audit-fixing handoff artifact.

**Does brief-tightness remain the load-bearing variable, or does cross-codebase variation introduce new variables?** PIC's frontend has high pattern-density (canonical patterns live in the same codebase as the fix targets, several times). A codebase with lower pattern-density may require additional disciplines beyond the session-06 list. If a repo-2 brief is tight by PIC's standards but produces ambiguity rates closer to F-1's three-per-run, the variable is the codebase, not the brief — and the addendum needs to name "pattern-density" as a co-dependent variable.

**Do the four agent-vs-brief disagreement shapes generalize, or are there shapes PIC didn't surface?** The taxonomy is provisional at four. Repo 2 may surface a fifth: agent disagreeing with the issue's premise rather than the brief's wording; agent disagreeing with the prior PR's framing; agent recognizing two issues should be merged into one. Any new shape would refine the taxonomy without invalidating it.

**Does the cleanup-then-refactor pattern apply outside of manifested-corruption scenarios?** PIC's two split-vs-bundle decisions (#99 and cluster B) both involved manifested production damage. Repo 2 may have issues with structural fixes but no manifested half — the diagnostic question presumes a manifested half exists. If repo 2 has issues that are structurally-only but still bundle-vs-split decisions (e.g., refactoring a function and renaming its callers — one PR or two?), the diagnostic needs extension or replacement.

**Does same-file non-adjacent parallelism hold under different code styles?** PIC's EditDrawer.jsx has clear sub-component boundaries that made the 500+ line buffer trivial to enforce. A codebase with frequent same-file edits at shared regions (imports, top-of-file types, a common utility section) would produce conflicts that git can't auto-merge even with nominally-separate target sections. If repo 2 has a tightly-coupled file shape, line-region independence may be necessary but not sufficient; the brief may need to specify the *minimum buffer size* and the agent may need to defer or relocate edits that touch shared regions.

**How does the three-layer fence behave on a codebase with different production topology?** PIC's production surface is managed Postgres, DigitalOcean Spaces, no message queue, no workflow engine. Repo 2's surface may include classes of risk the three layers don't catch. The fence's L1 deny rules are PIC-specific; the L2 skill-mediation is portable in shape but not in content; the L3 PR-description line is fully portable. If repo 2's production topology produces a risk class missed by all three layers, that argues for an L4 specific to the new topology, not for replacing the three-layer scheme.

## 7. Status of the methodology

The combined work — Phase 1 synthesis v1 + Phase 2 addendum v1 — is substantively more complete than the synthesis alone. The audit-to-autonomy pipeline now has both halves articulated: how to prepare a codebase for autonomous agents (auditing phase) and how to execute the resulting backlog with autonomous agents on the tractable subset (fixing phase). The two documents compose: the synthesis predicted the frontend would be the autonomous-agent track and named the cleanest entry; the addendum confirms the prediction with sixteen data points and articulates the methodology that made the prediction land.

Honest about caveats. The fixing-phase findings are no less PIC-specific than the auditing-phase findings. One orchestrator, one codebase, one model family, sixteen issues from one audit's `agent-friendly:yes` pool. The brief-tightness disciplines are calibrated against the patterns PIC's codebase happened to contain. The agent-vs-brief taxonomy is calibrated against the four shapes PIC happened to surface. The three-layer fence is calibrated against PIC's production topology. All of it is v1.

Repo 2 tests both layers. Until then, both remain v1-grade hypotheses — the synthesis and the addendum together. The next deliverable after this addendum is the public thesis articulation, which uses the audit-to-autonomy pipeline as its proof of concept. The deliverable after that is the artifact-update session, which folds Phase 2's findings into `docs/methodology/`. After both, repo 2 begins — and the next synthesis-plus-addendum pair will either confirm both layers, refine one or both, or surface enough new methodology to invalidate either's current articulation. The methodology survives v1 status only by being tested.
